Internet Access is a Fundamental Right- Spyros Kasapis

In Spyros Kasapis article, Internet Access is a Fundamental Right, he argues that internet access should be treated like a necessity, even going as far as calling it a human right, grouping it with things like food, education and healthcare. Particularly highlighting the social imbalance and divide it caused during the COVID-19 pandemic. The article references Salvador Allendes idea of how happiness is dependant on basic needs being met. He then follows up by arguing that in today’s digital world, internet access is just as important.

Kasapis references a 2020 decision by the Indian Supreme Court that recognized internet access as a constitutional right under Article 19 (freedom of expression). He uses this to show how as time goes by, more and more countries are going to slowly start viewing internet access as being important, going as far as making implementing it into their laws. He emphasizes how the pandemic forced many people to rely heavily on the internet to continue working remotely, online schooling, shop or just to communicate with other people. However, this led to people who didn’t have access to the internet being disadvantaged as they were left out of important parts of life, making existing inequalities even worse. .

In this article, Kasapis presents two ideas: internet access itself being a human right, or if it serves as a tool for people to exercise their rights, for example, freedom of speech and so on. While He recognizes this debate, he argues that based on the issues that arise from not having internet, especially in places like school or work, means that it should be treated as an essential.

Kasapis ends the article by asking two major questions about the future. Firstly, should governments make sure that everyone has access to the internet? Secondly, who exactly should oversee running the internet, and what rules should be followed. He believes that the internet should be managed in a way that ensures fairness among its users, with a strong focus on protecting human rights.

Kasapis aim in this article is to promote the view that internet access should be available to everyone globally, and that it helps ensure that human rights are maintained even in a digital society. His methodology is argumentative, and he draws on observations made, such as the COVID-19 pandemic. The Indian Supreme Court ruling and philosophical reflections (Allende). The results are to show that political actions need to be taken to recognize access to the internet as a human right and create rules for the fair use of it.

He makes a strong argument that was further backed by the COVID-19 pandemic, where digital inequalities were made very evident. However, the length of the content of the article makes it appear as though it lacks depth. He mentions how people like Jeff Bezos got richer while many others were disadvantaged from the lack of access to the internet, however he doesn’t explore systemic economic structures or suggest specific policies that can be put in place to help fix this issue.

Kasapis mostly shows the internet as a neutral and positive tool. Critics like Evgeny Morozov, in The Net Delusion (2011) however, argue that the internet can be used to promote freedom as well as having control and surveillance. This basically means that simply providing access isn’t enough, you also must factor in how free, open and fair the access is. The exploration of these factors would be greatly beneficial as it could remove the assumption that the internet is always a force for good.

While Kasapis briefly mentions internet governance, he doesn’t fully explore the different ways it can be managed. Scholars like Tim Wu (The Master Switch, 2010) argues that monopolies in the tech industry pose a serious threat to democracy. The use of this perspective could help deepen the discussion around how digital spaces should be structured and managed.

Future research could look at how exactly the access to the internet can be made available to everyone while still being affordable, including those in disadvantaged communities. It could also explore the different ways the internet can be managed. Another thing that could be investigated is also the potential dangers of giving everyone access to the internet. These dangers include things like the spread of misinformation, scams or online surveillance. Researchers should also investigate exploring more about how not having access to the internet really impacted people’s education and work life in the long run.

Kasapis article is a clear and convincing starting point for the idea that access to the internet should be considered a human right. Ideas from philosophy and politics are brought together and used an argument to further back his argument. However, it doesn’t tackle issues like who exactly should foresee the management of the internet, how economical systems like money and power can shape this access, as well as how technology can both be helpful and harmful.
