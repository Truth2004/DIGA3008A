What does an ethical internet look like

by Nhlanzeko Mwandla on 18 May 2025

In my opinion, an ethical internet is one where everyone is treated fairly, has equal access and is protected from harm. It’s a digital space where everyone’s privacy is respected, there is transparency in content with no misuse of information, everyone is given a voice with no discrimination and technology serves people, not corporations or authoritarian interests.

Drawing from Spyro Kasapis’s article,” Internet Access is a Fundamental Right”, access to the internet is no longer an optional thing to be connected to, it is essential for education, healthcare, and economic survival. This was further backed by using the Covid 19 pandemic as a source of reference on how exclusion from the internet deepened the divide of inequality between the rich and the poor, as it locked out marginalized communities out of critical services. In order for the internet to be considered ethical, access to it must be treated as a basic right instead of just a luxury, grouping it in with things like food, electricity, education and access to healthcare.

An ethical internet should also protect users’ data and privacy. Today’s internet thrives on surveillance where our data is harvested, sold and even weaponized without consent. The Web Foundation warns that unchecked corporate and government data collection destroys trust and autonomy. Users should always understand how their data is being used and should have a say in if whether they allow it or not. Informed consent should be the default and terms of services shouldn’t be hidden in a long list of documents with hard to read text that no one reads.

True accessibility means accounting for everyone using it. An ethical internet must actively consider diverse needs such as designing it for people with disabilities, language barriers and cultural backgrounds. People mustn’t be forced to adapt to such designs, but designs must be made easier to use and feel intuitive for users.

Kasapis poses a critical question, “Who governs the internet?”. In this moment in time, tech giants manage how we communicate, consume information and guides how we think about certain situations based on what they decided to show us or allow us to see. An ethical internet would ensure that this isn’t the case. Instead, it would be governed collectively, with rules shaped by the very people who use it.

Building this future, however, requires more than just policies being put in place, it requires actions to be taken. We must reject the idea that exploitation is the inevitable cost of convenience and instead demand that internet access aligns with human rights. Ultimately an ethical internet isn’t just about better technology but its about building a digital community that is accessible, fair and accountable to all.
