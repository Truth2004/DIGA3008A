The Politics of Digital Access- Reflective reading

The article utilizes an intersectional framework demonstrating how social inequalities intersect with forms of digital inequality. For example, it emphasizes the point that women tend to underperform their male counterparts even when they are equally skilled (Hargittai & Shafer, 2006). Women, socially conditioned to cater to patriarchal norms, often lack confidence in their abilities, something worse when it comes to specialization domains like technology.

The section on racial gaps shows us how digital exclusion tends to reflect real world inequalities. The author compares two different views, one that argues that the digital gap replicates already existing social hierarchies, while the other argues that digital tools can mitigate inequality by expanding social networks.

Another valuable contribution is the life course perspective that shows how digital inequalities grow over time. The author mentions how kids from poorer areas often lack access to fast internet or any, greatly limiting their ability to learn digital literacy. This leads to things like poor performance at school, leading to less job opportunities for them, and even health gaps in the new future. While richer people get a massive advantage like easier access to healthcare services and better job opportunities.

However, the article could delve deeper into how the mentioned inequalities are institutionalized. For example, public schools, especially those that aren’t on the privileged side, can’t afford digital tools to help students with digital literacy, while richer kids can afford their own digital tools and private tutors. This shows that digital inequality isn’t just about individual access but about systems failing certain marginalized communities of people.

The article sometimes focuses a little too much on individual factors contributing to digital inequality. While it acknowledges structural factors, it mainly highlights skills, usage patterns, and self-perception. This could give the idea that digital exclusion is more of a personal problem rather than a systemic one, ignoring issues like corporate monopolies over internet infrastructure, biased algorithms, or unaffordable broadband in low-income areas.

The author also sometimes acts like more digital access helps. They note that tech savvy workers earn more (DiMaggio and Bonikowski, 2008) or that eHealth improves care. But they ignore how digital tools can also enable abuse like exploitation such as health apps that commodify personal data. A sharper critique might ask something along the lines of: does digital inclusion really help marginalized groups, or just trap them in new forms of low wage and surveillance?

The article briefly touches up on global gaps, noting that Africa, despite containing 14% of the world’s population, accounts for only 3% of internet users (Fuchs & Horak, 2008). This imbalance stems from neocolonial systems where rich countries and big companies dominate internet access, costs, and content. The authors could have dug deeper into how western tech companies profit from the south while offering locals with limited, often censored connections.

They also miss resistance movements, like community run networks or open-source tools that battles corporate control. Including these perspectives would have provided more of a nuanced view of how marginalized groups are not victims of digital inequality, but they’re fighting back to reclaim technology.

In this article, Robison et al. (2025) convincingly shows how digital inequality is a critical social issue, intersecting with and reinforcing existing forms of oppression. However, their analysis could be further improved on by directly confronting the economic and political systems causing these inequalities. Digital inequality isn’t just about technology, but its about that technology by fair for everyone who uses it. While the article does address this, real change requires looking at the root causes, not just their results.
