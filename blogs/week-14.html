Designing for Inclusion

by Nhlanzeko Mwandla on 26 May 2025

When we create art or write code, we aren’t just being creative but we’re also making choices that reflect our values. These choices either support fairness or make inequality worse. As time goes by, art and technology start to mix in a more prevalent manner, this means that we have to be more mindful of how we use the two. In my blog I’m going to explain how I attempt to be both ethical and artistic in my code through representation, how accessible my coding practices are, and if whether it has fairness.

Art is often used to tell stories. In today’s time, technology has also been included in the meaning making process and is used as a tool to tell stories. However, with this being the case, a group of people is often left out or misrepresented through this. Questions like “whose stories are we telling and how are we going to tell them?” need to be asked by creators aiming to be ethical in their work. The first step is doing our outmost best in avoiding stereotypes and instead including diversity in the voices that speak out in storytelling through both art and technology. Mimi Onuohas “Library of Missing Datasets “explores how missing data can make an entire community appear invisible or non-existent. Most digital tools are often based on western ideas and beliefs of design. Researchers like Ramon Amaro talk about “abolitionist computation”, which means building tools that fight injustice instead of repeating it.

This same data however has been found to have biases. Joy Buolamwinis “Gender Shades” study showed that facial recognition doesn’t work as well on darker skin toned people, that’s because the data used is mostly from lighter skinned faces. When the code for this is hidden away from the public, it also makes it harder for people to call out these problems. Explaining your design choices can make your work more ethical.

Accessibility in my opinion must be held above all. It’s a major issue if people are unable to even use your platform because of their disability or lack of access you provide. A good design is descrived as one that includes everyone. The use of things like alt text, captioning your videos and making sure your work is easy to use with a keyboard or its responsive on smaller screens and screen readers can go a long way in promoting ethical coding.

These systems are only possible through the people that are brought in to create it. Giving credit where credit is due is important. If your work draws on other cultures or groups of people, acknowledging this shows respect as well as being responsible with your sources.

How your code is written can also have an impact on the environment. Making sure that you write efficient code that isn’t too power hungry allows you to save energy. Studies show that a single generative prompt consumes 3Wh of power. This may not seem like much but consider how many of these happen in a day.

When ethical practices are being mentioned, no ones expecting you to get everything right, just that you do more research into making your code as efficient as you can in the moment. By considering who its for, how its used and who benefits from it, we can make art and code support justice.

References

Buolamwini, Joy. Gender Shades, 2018.

Noble, Safiya Umoja. Algorithms of Oppression, 2018.

Onuoha, Mimi. The Library of Missing Datasets, 2016.
